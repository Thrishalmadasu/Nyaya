CASE: Maneka Gandhi v Union of India (1978)

KEY FACTS: The petitioner's passport was impounded by the Government "in the public interest" under the Passport Act, 1967, without furnishing any reasons or affording her a hearing. She challenged the action as violating Articles 14, 19 and 21.

PRINCIPLE: A "procedure established by law" under Article 21 must be right, just, fair and reasonable — not arbitrary, fanciful or oppressive; an unfair or unreasonable procedure is no procedure at all. Articles 14, 19 and 21 are not mutually exclusive but interlinked (the "golden triangle"), and a law depriving personal liberty must satisfy all three. This expansively reinterpreted personal liberty and overruled the narrow approach of A.K. Gopalan.

HOLDING: The Court read principles of natural justice into the statute, requiring reasons and a fair hearing; the entrenchment of fair, just and reasonable procedure became the lasting ratio.

COURT: Supreme Court of India
AREA: constitutional law — personal liberty, due process (Article 21)
