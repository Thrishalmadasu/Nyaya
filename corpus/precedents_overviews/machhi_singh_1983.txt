CASE: Machhi Singh v State of Punjab (1983)

KEY FACTS: Arising from a family feud, seventeen people were killed in a series of five revenge attacks across neighbouring Punjab villages in one night. Several accused were convicted; four were sentenced to death.

PRINCIPLE: Elaborating the "rarest of rare" doctrine of Bachan Singh, the Court identified categories that may justify a death sentence — the manner of commission (extremely brutal, diabolical), the motive (cold-blooded, for gain), socially abhorrent or anti-social nature, magnitude of the crime (multiple killings), and the personality of the victim (helpless child, woman, public figure). The court must balance aggravating against mitigating circumstances, with full weight to mitigation, and impose death only when life imprisonment is plainly inadequate.

HOLDING: Applying these guidelines, the death sentence was confirmed for those whose culpability fell within the rarest-of-rare category.

COURT: Supreme Court of India
AREA: criminal law — death penalty, rarest-of-rare guidelines
