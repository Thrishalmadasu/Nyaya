CASE: A.K. Gopalan v State of Madras (1950)

KEY FACTS: A.K. Gopalan, a Communist leader, was detained under the Preventive Detention Act, 1950. He challenged the detention as violating his fundamental rights under Articles 19, 21 and 22 of the Constitution.

PRINCIPLE: "Procedure established by law" in Article 21 means a procedure enacted by a competent legislature; it does not import the American "due process" standard, so courts could not test the fairness or reasonableness of that procedure. The Court further held that Articles 19, 21 and 22 are mutually exclusive and operate in separate compartments, each tested on its own terms.

HOLDING: The detention was upheld and the petition substantially dismissed. (This narrow, siloed reading of Article 21 was later overruled in Maneka Gandhi v Union of India.)

COURT: Supreme Court of India
AREA: constitutional law — preventive detention, personal liberty (Article 21)
