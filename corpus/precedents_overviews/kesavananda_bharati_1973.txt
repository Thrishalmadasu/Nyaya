CASE: Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala (1973)

KEY FACTS: The head of a religious mutt challenged Kerala land-reform laws restricting management of its property, and in the process the validity of the Constitution (Twenty-fourth, Twenty-fifth and Twenty-ninth Amendment) Acts came up for decision before a thirteen-judge bench.

PRINCIPLE: Parliament has wide power under Article 368 to amend any part of the Constitution, including fundamental rights, but that power does not extend to altering or destroying the "basic structure" or essential features of the Constitution. Features such as the supremacy of the Constitution, the rule of law, separation of powers, judicial review and the federal and democratic character form this inviolable core.

HOLDING: The Court overruled Golak Nath, upheld the amendments subject to the basic structure doctrine, and thereby established that doctrine as a permanent limit on the amending power.

COURT: Supreme Court of India (13-judge bench)
AREA: constitutional law — basic structure doctrine, amending power (Article 368)
