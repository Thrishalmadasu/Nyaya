CASE: Darshan Singh v State of Punjab (2010)

KEY FACTS: During a clash between closely related families over adjoining agricultural fields, the deceased's party and the accused confronted each other; the accused fired shots from a licensed gun, causing death, and pleaded the right of private defence.

PRINCIPLE: The Court comprehensively restated the principles governing the right of private defence: it is a defensive, not punitive, right available against a reasonable apprehension of danger to body or property; a person faced with imminent danger need not wait to be struck first or modulate his defence step by step; the right must be exercised in good faith and the force used must be proportionate to the apprehended threat; and whether the right was available is a question of fact on the totality of circumstances.

HOLDING: Applying these principles, the Court assessed whether the accused had a reasonable apprehension justifying the force used.

COURT: Supreme Court of India
AREA: criminal law — right of private defence (Sections 96–106 IPC)
